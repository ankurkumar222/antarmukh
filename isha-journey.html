My Spiritual Journey

Notes on Inner Engineering & the Isha Path

Not a philosophy to admire from a distance, but a working system — rules to live by, sādhanās to practice, and a single question underneath all of it: can the friction between how life is and how the mind wants it to be simply be dropped?

Organized from personal notes on Sadhguru's teachings — reference material for daily practice, not a public teaching text

सर्वं मम नियमःThe Root Teaching

Everything below branches from one recognition, repeated in a hundred forms across the notes: you don't control what happens. You control what happens in you when it happens.

"You cannot always choose what happens around you. But you can choose how you experience what happens." — the whole of Inner Engineering compressed into one sentence.

पञ्च नियमाःThe Five Rules of Inner Engineering

Five working principles — not beliefs to hold, but rules to actually run your inner life by. Each one dismantles a small piece of the illusion that the outside world is what's in charge of your experience.

  • Evening Samyama — timing still to be finalized

Kept here deliberately as corrections, not just additions — a reminder that not every practice that sounds spiritually appealing is one Sadhguru actually recommends doing unsupervised.

षट् साधनाःSix Sādhanās for Living

Concrete, repeatable practices — each one targeting a specific piece of unconscious identification. Open each to see the practice and what it's actually training.

देहद्वारम्The Body as the First Doorway

"If you cannot observe your physical body, how will you observe subtler dimensions of yourself?" The whole Haṭha Yoga structure in the notes exists to answer that question in practice.

🌀

Shakti Chalana Kriya

A structured energy practice — Nāda Kriya (mudra + sound), breath-ratio cycles across Chin/Chinmaya/ Ādi/Merudaṇḍa mudrās, Sukha Prāṇāyāma, and the Pañca Sahita Kriya organ sequence (below). Built to strengthen "the very foundations of the system," and deliberately taught alongside Śūnya, since holding full attention on a 40–60 minute breath sequence takes years to develop.

This is a structured practice traditionally learned under a certified Isha teacher — noted here for personal reference, not as a self-teaching guide.
✨

Shambhavi Mahāmudrā

Mahāmudrā — a "seal" that locks and redirects energy that would otherwise dissipate through sensory reaction. Every sensory engagement normally costs energy; the seal reverses that into a net gain. The notes cite two measured effects: a significantly higher Cortisol Awakening Response (greater wakefulness) and elevated BDNF (supporting cognitive health) in practitioners — with 90 days of consistent practice cited as enough to meaningfully shift baseline wakefulness.

🦴

Yoga Namaskar & the Spine

The spine treated not as a bone structure but as the foundation of perception and communication in the system. A seven-step sequence built to keep it flexible — preventing collapse with age, easing nerve compression, and unblocking emotional stagnation. This is named directly as a path toward citta-vṛtti-nirodha — Patañjali's phrase for the stilling of the mind's own fluctuations.

👁️

Ākāśī Mudrā

A perception-sharpening technique: locating a point of convergence roughly 17–19 inches from the brow center, at an 11-degree angle, and holding vision there. The stated aim isn't to see anything specific — only to strengthen the etheric element (ākāśa) and, through it, the raw capacity to perceive.

🧘

21 Āsanas — the Preparatory Set

Of 84 classical postures, 21 are used as the entry set, split into standing, prone, supine, and seated groups. Their function is explicitly preparatory: readying the "physical" and "karmic/mental" bodies before any real energy work, since boosting the energy body without that readiness risks strain. Bhūta Śuddhi — elemental purification — works alongside this, harmonizing the system with air, fire, earth, and water before the boundary between "self" and "surroundings" is allowed to thin.

🖐️

Releasing Karma Through the Body

A simpler daily practice for when formal Haṭha Yoga isn't available: eyes closed, gently stretch one hand, observing every sensation without judgment; once familiar, extend this to the whole body, allowing subtle, non-mechanical movement. The stated aim is releasing chemical deposits that accumulate at the body's 114 chakras, joints, and nodes — karma is described as operating on mind, chemistry, and raw sensation, which is why willpower alone rarely breaks a habit that has become physiological.

कर्मबीजानिKarma — From Seed to Freedom

Not reward and punishment from an external ledger-keeper — karma, in these notes, is simply the mechanism by which unconscious action becomes tomorrow's compulsion.

StageYogic TermMeaning
SeedSaṃskāraA stored impression, left by action plus emotion
SproutVāsanāA tendency or urge arising from that impression
TreeHabit / ActionRepeated behavior, grown from the tendency
FruitExperiencePleasure, pain, success, suffering — the harvest
New seedsFresh saṃskārasThe fruit itself plants the next cycle

Three Intensifiers

Repetition deepens the groove. Emotion — fear, desire, anger, attachment — plants seeds fast and deep. Identification — the moment a thought becomes "this is me" — hardens the seed into something that resists change. A single angry reaction leaves a light imprint; years of anger plus justification build a strong saṃskāra.

Karma as Old Software

Written unconsciously, continuously updated, running habits and reactions without asking permission. The starting point of freedom is simply recognizing the software as software — not identity. Once seen, it can be rewritten; while unseen, it only repeats.

The Psychosomatic Loop

Every mental fluctuation triggers a chemical reaction; the chemical reaction produces a physical sensation; the sensation reinforces the original fluctuation. This loop — not just belief or willpower — is what makes a pattern feel like "just who I am." Thinking of mountains produces one chemical reaction; thinking of tigers produces another — even minor fluctuations compound into real physical consequence over time.

Karma Samyama

Sustained, silent awareness applied to action itself — seeing clearly why you act, not only what you do. Fully seen karma cannot blindly repeat; without emotional fuel, old seeds burn out on their own. The working sentence: "I am not punished by karma. I am educated by it."

Three Kinds of Karmic Seed

  • Dormant seeds — not active, waiting for the right situation; a hidden fear that only surfaces under real pressure
  • Sprouting seeds — becoming thoughts, urges, desires; the felt sense of "I don't know why, but I feel like doing this"
  • Fruiting seeds — already shaping life events: relationships, challenges, talents, obstacles already in motion

You don't control which seeds sprout. You do control whether you water them.

Why Samskaras Feel Like "Personality"

Because they run automatically. Personality is stored memory in motion — free will exists, but it operates inside already-conditioned patterns. That's why two people can face the identical event: one reacts calmly, one explodes. Same situation, different samskaric soil underneath.

The Momentum of a Karmic Cycle

The mind works like a ripple — one small fluctuation generates momentum that can persist a lifetime. Worry is the clearest example: at 18, people worry about the future; at 70, people still worry, even though most of life is already behind them. The specific worry changes; the underlying momentum of mental fluctuation → chemical reaction → sensation → further fluctuation does not, unless it's deliberately interrupted.

What Karma Samyama Is NOT

Not thinking about past mistakes. Not over-analyzing life events. Not feeling guilty, and not feeling proud. It happens only when the mind is genuinely steady and silent — no silence, no samyama. This distinction matters: replaying the past in thought is the opposite of the practice, not a version of it.

"Action done in awareness leaves no chain behind." — karma stops binding the moment it stops being unconscious.

Why Karma Samyama Leads to Liberation — Step by Step

  1. Karma binds only when it is unconscious — automatic action creates new karmic seeds.
  2. Karma Samyama brings full awareness to action — you see clearly why you act, not just what you do.
  3. Seen karma loses its power — what is fully seen cannot blindly repeat.
  4. No new seeds are created — action still happens, but without attachment or ego behind it.
  5. Old seeds burn out naturally — without emotional fuel, they simply fade.

Result: life continues, but the bondage ends. "Burning seeds in the fire of awareness" — not suppression, not a fight. Seeing itself is what dissolves them.

The Five Kośas & Karma's Reach

Karma has a grip on the physical (annamaya), mental (manomaya), and energetic (prāṇamaya) layers of existence — but loses that grip entirely in the etheric (vijñānamaya) and bliss (ānandamaya) layers. Freedom, in this framing, isn't erasing karma — it's stepping into the two dimensions cause-and-effect was never able to reach in the first place.

Cosmic Rhythm

The notes distinguish a stable solar cycle (harmony, when aligned with) from shorter lunar and 3–6-month cycles, which — repeated unconsciously — tend toward psychological instability. Yogic practice is framed partly as a re-alignment toward the steadier rhythm.

A useful daily-use sentence straight from the notes: "This reaction is a saṃskāra showing itself, not the full truth of who I am." Repeated during stress, it creates space almost instantly.

अष्टाङ्गयोगःThe Eight Limbs, and Where Samyama Lives

A progressive inward journey — from how you live among people, to how awareness finally rests in itself, needing nothing further.

1
Yama — social ethics
Ahiṃsā, satya, asteya, brahmacharya, aparigraha

The outer discipline — how you relate to others. The behavioral foundation everything else is built on.

2
Niyama — personal observance
Śauca, santoṣa, tapas, svādhyāya, Īśvara-praṇidhāna

The inner discipline — how you relate to yourself. Inner alignment before any technique is introduced.

3
Āsana — posture
A steady, comfortable body

The goal is stability, not gymnastics — preparing the body simply to be able to sit.

4
Prāṇāyāma — breath regulation
Balancing energy and the nervous system

Prepares the mind, the way āsana prepared the body.

5
Pratyāhāra — withdrawal of senses
The bridge between outer and inner yoga

The senses keep working — eyes still see, ears still hear — but attention stops being dragged outward by them. Not suppression, not force: like a turtle drawing in its legs, then extending them again when safe. This is non-engagement, not resistance — and without it, concentration simply leaks.

6
Dhāraṇā — concentration
Effort is still present

Holding attention on one object — breath, mantra, chakra, even karma itself.

7
Dhyāna — meditation
Effort thins out

Continuous, uninterrupted flow of attention — no breaks, no jumping between objects.

8
Samādhi — absorption
No effort, only knowing

Observer, object, and process merge; only the object shines in awareness.

Samyama

When dhāraṇā, dhyāna, and samādhi are applied together, seamlessly, to the same object, Patañjali calls this combined state samyama. It is explicitly a tool, not a starting point — placed last in the sequence for a reason: without yama-niyama it amplifies ego; without pratyāhāra, concentration leaks before it can even begin. Ethical → physiological → psychological → meditative → absorptive — the order is the safeguard.

Sādhana

What you do to transform yourself — discipline, meditation, breath, self-observation. Inner mastery through conscious practice. Sādhana trains the mind and the body.

Upāsanā

How you relate to the divine — devotion, chanting, offering, surrender. Upāsanā softens the heart. Sādhana without upāsanā can turn dry; upāsanā without sādhana can stay merely emotional. The tradition asks for both: sharpness and softness together.

वेदना द्वारम्Sensation — The Doorway Every Tradition Agrees On

Before the cross-tradition synthesis, the notes ground this in something very concrete: sensation is the actual bridge between you and life.

Why "Entry Point Is Sensation"

Everything known about the world and the self arrives through sensation. Sight is the sensation of light on the eyes. Sound is vibration on the ears. Emotion is chemicals producing an inner sensation. Even thought generates a subtle bodily sensation. Without sensation, nothing would be known at all — it is the bridge between you and life, not one channel among several.

What Observing It Reveals

Watching sensation directly, without judgment, surfaces the body (how it moves, breathes, reacts), emotion (which is just sensation carrying a story), thought (every thought creates some bodily reaction), and the whole inner experience — energy, calm, agitation, clarity, joy. This is what Sadhguru calls "the life process inside": heartbeat, breath, warmth, tension, release, tingling, thoughts rising and falling — an entire internal universe available only through sensation.

The Two-Minute Exercise

  1. Sit quietly for two minutes, eyes closed.
  2. Bring attention to the body.
  3. Notice any sensation — tingling, warmth, coolness, heartbeat, breath, pressure, vibration.
  4. Don't name it. Don't judge it. Just experience it.

Can be done anywhere — office, home, walking. Awareness measurably increases within a few days of repetition.

What Actually Happens

Mind slows down. Emotional reactions reduce. Anxiety reduces. Inner clarity increases. Living in memory and imagination stops, and living here and now becomes natural — which is why Sadhguru describes observing sensation as, quite literally, observing life.

Building It Into a Daily Habit

Morning (3–5 min): on waking, feel the body's contact with the surface, the natural breath, sensations in face/chest/hands. During the day (30–60 sec): attach it to existing habits — before opening the laptop, before a difficult message, while walking to the pantry, or whenever an emotion rises, ask "where do I feel this in my body?" Night (2–3 min): before sleep, scan head to toe for tension, relaxation, subtle vibration, then close with: "the day is over, sensations came and went, thoughts came and went, I remain as the one who knows."

This single practice, the notes point out, is not separate from the great traditions — it's the same instruction nine of them arrived at independently, in entirely different language. Tap a tradition.

Sensation → Awareness → Freedom. Every time attention returns to what's actually happening in the body — not the story about it — the same doorway that the Upanishads, the Gītā, Patañjali, the Buddha, Ramaṇa, Kashmir Śaivism, the Siddhars, and Vivekānanda all separately pointed toward opens again.
Reminder lines to rotate through the day: "I am the knower of this sensation." · "This too is passing through the field." · "Let me feel, not fight." · "Observe, don't judge." · "Return to the body. Return to now."

ब्रह्मचर्यम्Brahmacharya — Conscious Energy

One of the more demanding disciplines in the notes, treated with real nuance rather than blanket prohibition.

Why It's Emphasized

Advaita holds that realizing Brahman requires a mind that is clear and undistracted. Restlessness generated by unexamined desire (vikṣepa) works directly against the steadiness self-inquiry needs — so the discipline isn't about morality, it's about the specific mental clarity that deep contemplation requires.

Ojas & Tejas

In this framework, vital energy conserved through restraint is described as transmuting into ojas — a subtler strength sharpening intellect, memory, and will. Śaṅkarācārya's own Vivekacūḍāmaṇi names brahmacharya as foundational to jñāna: without this self-restraint, the mind stays impure enough that truth remains out of reach.

Householder vs. Renunciate

The notes are careful to distinguish these: for a householder (gṛhastha), brahmacharya means moderation and conscious relationship, not literal celibacy. For a monk (sannyāsī), it is closer to complete restraint, since worldly life itself has already been set aside.

The Practical Core

Less about suppression, more about redirection: noticing thoughts rather than being run by them, reducing unnecessary triggers, and channeling the same energy into focused, creative, or contemplative work. The discipline is framed as building capacity, not merely restricting behavior.

समस्या-समाधानम्A Working Template — Problem to Practice

The notes include a simple, repeatable format for turning any struggle into a concrete practice — worth keeping as a template, independent of the specific examples.

The Four-Question Format

  1. What problem am I actually facing? — named plainly, without dramatizing it.
  2. What's the real solution — not a vague intention, but a specific practice?
  3. When exactly does it happen — what time, what trigger?
  4. How will I know it's working?

Applied in the notes to energy dips, food timing, communication skill, and time management — each resolved into one or two concrete daily actions rather than a resolution to "try harder." The closing reminder attached to this section is worth keeping close: "You do not rise to the level of your goals. You fall to the level of your systems." — James Clear, quoted alongside Churchill's line on the courage to continue.

शून्यम्Śūnya — Where the Outer World Ends

Everything above eventually points here — the practice the notes name as the actual top priority, above every other sādhanā.

Śūnya asks for something specific: close the eyes, and let the only things that exist be breath, heartbeat, and the workings of prāṇa within. Everything outside becomes, in that moment, only imagery — not because the world is unreal, but because it is simply not where this particular attention is being placed.

"Move your attention to how life works within you. If you make it your number one priority, everything within you will organize itself accordingly."

The notes are direct about what happens without this: socially you may still get somewhere; physically the body proceeds toward the grave regardless; but the mind, left unfocused, only goes round and round. Only turning attention to what's fundamental — life as it is actually happening, right now, inside — produces real movement. Everything else in this document is scaffolding built to support exactly this one, simple, repeated turning inward.

सर्वं मम नियमः

"All rules in this existence are my rules."

Not a claim that the world bends to you — a recognition that your inner experience is entirely self-authored, so the only rules that actually govern your life are the ones you write within yourself. Everything else — the daily rhythm, the six sādhanās, the body work, the karma model, the eight limbs, the return to sensation — is simply the long, patient practice of learning to write those rules well.
