पातञ्जल योगसूत्राणि

Samādhi Pāda · Sūtras 1.1 – 1.10

A restless mind is not a flaw to be punished — it is a chariot without a trained charioteer. Patañjali's opening sūtras are not philosophy for its own sake; they are a driving manual, written for anyone who has ever been dragged somewhere by their own thoughts.

Organized as a deep commentary — the classical images (chariot, lake, storm) held intact, unfolded for an adult reader

अथ योगानुशासनम्Where the Text Begins

Patañjali doesn't open with a claim about the universe, the way the Upanishads do. He opens with a single word aimed directly at the reader.

अथ योगानुशासनम् ॥ १.१ ॥
Atha yoga-anuśāsanam
"Now begins the disciplined teaching of Yoga."

The word atha — "now" — is doing more work than any other word in the text. It isn't a scene-setter; it's a demand for readiness. Classical commentators read it as marking the moment a student has done enough living, enough failing, enough wanting, to finally be prepared to hear something difficult. Yoga is not reserved for renunciates or the already-calm — it is offered the instant anyone is ready to stop postponing. And anuśāsanam — discipline, systematic training, not punishment — tells you what kind of book you're holding: not inspiration, but a method, meant to be practiced the way one learns music or a craft, through repetition rather than a single moment of insight. Patañjali is explicit elsewhere that he isn't inventing this — the prefix anu ("continuing") marks him as compiler and systematizer of a far older oral tradition, connected closely to Sāṅkhya philosophy: where Sāṅkhya supplies the map of reality — puruṣa, prakṛti, the layers of mind — Yoga supplies the practice of walking it.

चित्तम् त्रिविधम्The Chariot of the Mind

Before the sūtras can be understood, the machinery they're describing needs a name. The tradition hands us an old image: body as chariot, senses as horses, mind as reins, discernment as charioteer, and the true Self as the one being carried. Touch each part.

The Red Car, Rushing Toward You

Watch the three lower parts work in half a second. A car rushes toward you. Manas registers red, sound, speed, and panics: something fast — what do I do? Buddhi — if it's trained — stays quiet underneath the noise and issues one clear order: move. The body obeys before thought finishes. Only afterward does Ahaṃkāra arrive, breathless, narrating: that car was coming for me — I saved myself. Nothing in this sequence required a self to be in danger, or a self to have acted — reflex and buddhi did the work — yet ahaṃkāra claims authorship anyway. This is the entire machinery of ordinary suffering in miniature: a fast, confused collector; a quiet decision-maker who rarely gets to drive; and a loud narrator that turns every event into a story about "me."

दश सूत्राणिTen Sūtras — Full Commentary

Each sūtra is compressed almost to the point of riddle — that's intentional; they were meant to be unpacked by a teacher, not read silently. Open each in turn.

पञ्च वृत्तयःThe Five Vṛttis

Sūtra 1.6 makes a striking claim: however many thousands of thoughts move through a mind in a day, every single one sorts into exactly five categories. Learning to sort is itself most of the practice.

अभ्यासवैराग्याभ्याम्The Two Wings of Stillness

Sūtra 1.12 answers the practical question every seeker eventually asks: how, concretely, do the waves actually stop? With exactly two tools, used together.

अभ्यास — Abhyāsa
Practice, sustained without break

Not a single strong effort but a returning — coming back to the discipline again and again, especially after slipping. Patañjali's own commentators specify three conditions: practiced over long time (dīrgha-kāla), without interruption (nairantarya), and with reverence (satkāra) — genuine seriousness rather than mechanical repetition. The measure of practice was never "did I do this perfectly" — it was always "did I return." A five-day streak broken on day six is not failure; failing to return on day seven is the only real failure the text recognizes.

वैराग्य — Vairāgya
Non-attachment to outcome

Not indifference and not suppression — vairāgya is holding effort tightly while holding the result lightly. "I must come first" manufactures fear with every setback; "I will give my best and stay calm" manufactures resilience instead. This is not detachment from the world, but detachment from the mind's habit of gripping outcomes it was never fully in control of in the first place. Effort belongs to you. Results only partly do.

श्रद्धावीर्यस्मृतिसमाधिप्रज्ञापूर्वकThe Five-Step Ladder to a Steady Mind

Sūtra 1.20 lays out how a steady mind actually forms — not as an event, but as a sequence that has to be climbed in order, and can be re-climbed after every fall.

1
श्रद्धा — Śraddhā
Faith — "I believe this path can help me."

Not belief without evidence, but a working trust that makes the first step possible before any results exist to point to. Since mind-work offers no scoreboard and no daily medal, this trust is what keeps a person moving when progress is invisible.

2
वीर्य — Vīrya
Energy — "I will put in effort, even when it's hard."

Faith without energy stays an opinion. Vīrya is the will that shows up even at partial strength — not requiring motivation to already be high, only requiring the decision to begin anyway.

3
स्मृति — Smṛti
Memory — "I will remember why I started."

Every sustained practice hits a day where faith and energy both go quiet. Smṛti is the return to the original reason — not manufacturing new motivation, but retrieving the motivation that was already sufficient the first time.

4
समाधि — Samādhi
Absorption — "Doing it has become natural."

What began as effortful now happens without the earlier friction. This is not yet the technical samādhi of the later sūtras, but its everyday cousin — the state where practice stops requiring willpower because it has become the mind's default channel.

5
प्रज्ञा — Prajñā
Wisdom — "I can now tell what's real from what's noise."

The end product of the climb is not calm for its own sake, but discernment — a mind clear enough to correctly sort the important from the distracting, which is exactly the skill the Five Vṛttis above were training all along.

त्रिगुणाःThe Three Guṇas — Weather of the Mind

Alongside the ladder, the tradition names three recurring qualities every mind cycles through — not flaws to eliminate, but weather to recognize.

☀️

Sattva — the Sunny Mind

Calm, clear, quietly luminous — like a still lake reflecting the sky. This is the state in which thinking is accurate and decisions are wise. Not a permanent achievement, but a recurring weather pattern to be recognized and returned to.

🌊

Rajas — the River Mind

Fast, active, restless — the state of wanting to move, build, act, and talk. Neither good nor bad in itself; it is the energy that gets things done, but left unchecked it becomes the very agitation Sūtra 1.2 asks the practitioner to still.

🪨

Tamas — the Rock Mind

Heavy, dull, inert — sleep, sluggishness, the wish not to move at all. Necessary for rest and genuine recovery, but dangerous when mistaken for peace, since dullness and stillness are not the same thing.

The Skill Is Not Elimination — It's Recognition

All three arise in every mind, often within a single day — sunny in the morning, riverlike by afternoon, rocky by night. None of them needs to be fought. The entire practical skill the tradition teaches is noticing which weather is currently active, and choosing the next action accordingly — water, breath, and movement for a Rock Mind; stillness and breath for a River Mind; nothing at all for a Sunny Mind, since it needs no correction.

संस्कारमार्गाःThe Paths Beneath the Field

Sūtra 1.6's companion teaching on viparyaya — wrong knowledge — explains why a person's habits and temperament feel so fixed, and exactly why they aren't.

The Rope and the Snake

A glistening, curved shape in low light triggers a single wrong perception — snake — and that one error cascades into a full storm of vṛttis: racing thoughts about venom, about death, about people once read of dying from snakebite, each new thought more convincing than the last, none of them checked against reality. Only when the breathing slows enough for buddhi to actually look does the shape resolve: it was a rope. Viparyaya — wrong knowledge — is exactly this: a mind that builds a convincing false picture and then reacts to the picture as if it were the fact.

Saṃskāra — the Worn Path

Every repeated thought leaves a trace — a saṃskāra — the way walking the same line through a field again and again wears a visible path. Repeat a fearful thought, an angry thought, a lazy thought often enough, and the mind starts finding that channel first, automatically, before any other option is considered. This is why temperament feels fixed: "he can't help it, that's just his nature" describes a well-worn path, not an unchangeable law. And a worn path can be walked less, while a new one is deliberately worn beside it — one counter-thought, one paused breath, one on-time start at a time. Change is called difficult for good reason: it works against gravity already established. But the text is explicit that difficult is not the same as impossible.

मैत्रीकरुणामुदितोपेक्षाणाम्Four Attitudes for a Calm Mind

Sūtra 1.33 gives the most immediately practical instruction in the whole chapter — four specific stances toward other people's fortune, plus one anchor beyond all of them, that keep the lake from turning stormy in the first place.

🤍

Maitrī — toward the happy

Friendliness toward those who are flourishing. Feelings spread — proximity to genuine cheerfulness lightens a mind, the way proximity to complaint weighs one down. Choosing company deliberately is itself a practice.

💧

Karuṇā — toward the suffering

Compassion toward those in pain — presence, kind words, small help — without requiring oneself to become sad in turn. Care and calm are not opposites; the steadiest people in a crisis are usually the most useful.

🎉

Muditā — toward the successful

Gladness at another's achievement, rather than the quiet subtraction jealousy performs — why not me. Someone else's success was never actually taken from your own; muditā is simply refusing the story that says otherwise.

⚖️

Upekṣā — toward the wrongdoer

Equanimity toward those behaving badly — not approval, and not passivity, but declining to let hatred take the wheel. Wrong can be named and stopped firmly without the mind itself catching fire in the process.

Īśvara-praṇidhāna — the Fifth Anchor

Beyond the four social attitudes sits one more: remembering something larger than the immediate storm. When the mind is genuinely overwhelmed, the text offers a physical anchor rather than an argument — the breath, and the sound of Om. Three slow repetitions of A-U-M are enough to measurably change breathing rate and settle the nervous system, not through belief but through the simple mechanics of a slowed exhale. The remembering itself is the practice: this storm, like every other one, will pass.

स्वाध्याय-परीक्षाActive Recall — Test the Understanding

योगश्चित्तवृत्तिनिरोधः

Yogaś citta-vṛtti-nirodhaḥ — "Yoga is the stilling of the mind's movements."

Ten sūtras, and the whole architecture is already visible: a mind with layers, a charioteer worth training, waves worth watching rather than chasing, paths worth re-walking on purpose, and a handful of concrete stances toward other people that keep the lake from ever needing to storm at all. Nothing here asks for perfection. It asks only for one thing Sūtra 1.1 already named: to begin now, and to keep returning.
